# Hooks

The hook is the first 1 to 3 seconds (and the first line of on-screen text). It
carries more weight than any other part of the piece: if it fails, nothing else
gets seen. Treat hook-writing as the main event, not a warm-up.

## How to generate a strong set

- Produce **6 to 10 hooks across different archetypes below**, not 6 rewordings
  of one. The point is to give the user real angles to choose from.
- Lead with the **payoff or the tension**, never with setup, throat-clearing, or
  "hey guys."
- Make the first line **concrete**: a number, a named thing, a stakes word.
- Open a **loop** the viewer needs closed (curiosity), or state a **stake** they
  can't ignore (consequence).
- Say it out loud. If it takes longer than about 2 seconds or stumbles, cut it
  down.
- Label each hook with its archetype so the user learns the pattern.

## Archetypes

1. **Curiosity gap.** Name the existence of an answer without giving it.
   *"Nobody tells you the one thing that actually decides whether this works."*
   Use when the payoff is genuinely non-obvious. Don't fake the gap.

2. **Withhold / product-tease.** Show the result or the effect, hold back the
   product or method. *"I'm not going to say the brand, but my [result] is
   unreal."* This is the dominant save-driving mechanic: viewers stay to find out
   what it is, and the comments fill with "what is it?" Use when the reveal is the
   value. (Don't withhold so long it feels like bait.)

3. **Stakes / consequence.** Frame a cost of not watching. *"Stop doing [common
   thing] if you actually want [outcome]."* Use for advice or education.

4. **Bold or contrarian claim.** Stake a position against the consensus.
   *"Morning routines are why you're tired. Here's the fix."* Must be defensible
   in the payoff, or trust dies.

5. **Direct call-out.** Name the exact viewer. *"If you have [specific
   situation], this is for you."* Narrows reach but spikes relevance and saves.

6. **Mistake / cost confession.** *"I wasted $2,000 and 6 months so you don't
   have to."* Specificity (the number) does the work.

7. **Number / listicle.** *"3 things I wish I knew before [thing]."* Sets a clear
   container, and pairs well with fast pacing and on-screen counters.

8. **In media res.** Start mid-action or mid-result, explain after. Good for
   demos and before/afters where the visual *is* the hook.

9. **Question / POV.** *"Why does no one talk about [surprising thing]?"* Use
   sparingly. It's weaker than a claim unless the question is genuinely sharp.

10. **Social proof / result-first.** *"Everyone keeps asking how I did this."*
    Borrows momentum from implied demand.

## Anti-patterns (reject these)

- Slow build-ups, intros, logos, or "hey guys, welcome back."
- Brand-first ("Check out our new …"). Lead with the viewer's outcome.
- Vague benefit ("level up your content"), with no concrete noun or stake.
- A hook that the body never pays off. It's the fastest way to lose saves and trust.
- Cleverness that needs explaining. Clarity outperforms wit.

## Pairing hooks to payoff

Every hook makes a promise. Before finalizing, check the script actually delivers
on it within the first few seconds of the body, and fully by the payoff. If it
can't, change the hook, not the truth.

## Constructing the hook line

Archetypes (above) tell you *what kind* of hook to reach for; this is *how to
build the actual line*.

- **Shock, then intrigue, then satisfy.** Open on a visual pattern interrupt or a
  surprising line (shock), open a curiosity gap (intrigue), and signal a payoff is
  coming (satisfy). The viewer should feel "I have to see where this goes."
- **The curiosity loop, in three moves:**
  1. *Context lean-in.* One beat that makes the topic clear ("Everyone overpays
     for coffee gear…").
  2. *Scroll-stop interjection*, often the word **"but"**, to snap attention
     ("…but the cheap one actually wins").
  3. *Contrarian snapback.* A twist that reverses the expectation and opens the
     loop the body will close.
- **Staccato open.** Short, rapid sentences in the first seconds. High value *per
  second* beats smooth prose, so cut connective words.
- **Work on mute and on sound.** Put a **3 to 5 word** bold text hook on screen in
  the first frame, *and* say the hook out loud (spoken audio is indexed for search;
  see `platforms.md`). Pair it with rapid motion in the opening shot.
